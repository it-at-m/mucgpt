# Product

## Register

product

## Product Summary

MUCGPT is an AI assistant platform for municipal work. It gives employees a reliable place to use language models for writing, reviewing, summarizing, structuring, and reusing text-based work.

The product is built for a broad public-sector audience: from people using AI for the first time to technical power users who already know many AI tools. MUCGPT should be approachable enough for occasional users, but capable enough that experienced users prefer it over generic external chat tools.

Its core product object is the **assistant**: a reusable, workflow-specific chat setup with its own instructions, behavior, optional tools, and sharing scope. Chat is the execution surface; assistants are the product structure that makes MUCGPT useful for real recurring work.

## Users

MUCGPT is primarily for employees of municipal organizations. It should serve all roles and departments, not only technical, administrative, or management users.

Primary users include:

- Employees who need help drafting, rewriting, checking, simplifying, or summarizing text.
- Employees who use recurring templates, forms, or standard communication patterns.
- Employees who want a guided assistant for a specific task instead of starting from an empty chat.
- Power users who create specialized assistants for their own workflows or teams.

Secondary users include:

- Assistant creators who configure prompts, tools, examples, quick starters, models, and behavior.
- Organizational power users who publish assistants for selected departments or wider internal use.
- Future administrative users who need visibility into shared assistants and can correct, adjust, or remove inappropriate assistant configurations.

## Product Purpose

MUCGPT exists to make AI useful inside municipal workflows without turning users into prompt engineers.

The product should help employees:

- Move from a work need to a useful text result with minimal friction.
- Use AI for common language tasks such as drafting, reviewing, summarizing, simplifying, brainstorming, and filling or adapting templates.
- Create assistants that encode recurring instructions, tone, constraints, tools, and context.
- Reuse and share assistants across relevant departments or work groups.
- Understand what an assistant is configured to do before relying on it.

The most important product moment is not simply receiving an answer from a chat. The strongest “aha” moment should come from discovering or creating an assistant that fits a real recurring task and makes the next similar task easier.

## Assistants

An assistant is a workflow-specific chat configuration, not just a saved prompt.

Each assistant is centered on a system prompt that defines its role, behavior, constraints, and task focus. Assistants may also include tools, examples, quick starters, model settings, and metadata that help users understand how and when to use them.

Users should be able to create assistants for their own work. Assistants can be shared with selected departments or audiences when they are useful beyond the creator’s private workflow.

Published assistants do not imply full automation or organizational approval by default. The interface should make ownership, scope, and configuration transparent so users can judge whether an assistant is appropriate for their task.

## Trust & Control

MUCGPT should feel transparent, controllable, and accountable.

Users should be able to understand:

- What an assistant is intended to do.
- Who created or maintains it.
- Where it is meant to be used.
- Which tools or capabilities it may use.
- Whether it is private, shared, or broadly visible.
- What model or configuration choices affect its behavior, where relevant.

The product should support human-in-the-loop work by default. MUCGPT should help users produce, inspect, adapt, and decide. It should not silently execute unrestricted autonomous workflows.

Cost awareness, model transparency, and clearer ownership metadata are important long-term product qualities, even where they are not exposed in every surface yet.

## Brand Personality

Trustworthy, focused, approachable, modern.

MUCGPT should feel like a high-quality public-sector work tool, not like legacy administrative software. It should combine the reliability, accessibility, and seriousness expected in municipal environments with the speed and clarity users expect from strong modern AI products.

The tone should be practical and confident. It should avoid both bureaucratic heaviness and playful AI novelty.

## Differentiation

MUCGPT should be better than generic AI chat products for municipal work because it can adapt to organizational workflows, shared assistants, and approved internal capabilities.

The long-term advantage is not only access to language models. It is the ability to connect assistants to trusted internal tools, documents, processes, and reusable task patterns while keeping users in control.

MUCGPT should remain open-source friendly: the product concept should work for the City of Munich and for other public-sector organizations that want to adapt it to their own environment.

## Anti-References

MUCGPT should not feel like an enterprise admin console: dense, bureaucratic, table-first, or visually heavy just because it serves an organization.

It should not be a direct ChatGPT, Gemini, or generic AI chatbot clone. Familiar AI interaction patterns are welcome when they help users, but assistant creation, workflow specificity, sharing, and organizational context should give MUCGPT its own product identity.

MUCGPT should not become:

- A fully autonomous multi-agent system.
- An unrestricted automation platform.
- A hidden background executor for tools or workflows.
- A prompt-template library where assistants feel secondary.
- A decorative startup SaaS interface that feels detached from municipal work.

Human review, user intent, and transparent assistant behavior should remain central.

## Design Principles

1. Assistants are the product: chat is the execution surface, but assistant discovery, creation, configuration, sharing, and reuse should lead the experience.
2. Useful for everyone: the same product must work for first-time AI users and experienced power users without forcing either group into the other’s workflow.
3. Workflow before model mechanics: expose prompts, tools, creativity, examples, and models in the language of the user’s job whenever possible.
4. Confidence through transparency: show what an assistant can do, who owns it, where it applies, and what capabilities it uses before asking users to rely on it.
5. Human in the loop: MUCGPT helps users think, write, inspect, and decide. It should not hide autonomous execution behind friendly chat UI.
6. Municipal clarity, modern quality: make the product feel current without sacrificing accessibility, reliability, or operational seriousness.
7. Open-source adaptability: avoid product language that only works for one deployment. The core model should be reusable by other public-sector organizations.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. The interface should be keyboard usable, screen-reader friendly, reduced-motion friendly, and readable in typical municipal workplace environments.

Color must not be the only carrier of meaning. Interactive controls need visible focus states, adequate contrast, clear labels, and predictable behavior across light and dark themes.

The product must support a wide range of confidence levels with AI. It should guide inexperienced users without slowing down power users who know what they want to do.

## Success Measures

MUCGPT is successful when employees choose it as their preferred AI assistant platform for municipal work.

Signals of success include:

- Growing active usage across departments and roles.
- High user satisfaction and recurring qualitative feedback.
- Users creating and reusing assistants for real workflows.
- Shared assistants becoming useful beyond their original creator.
- Reduced reliance on generic external AI tools for municipal tasks.

A poor outcome would be a technically functional product that users avoid because generic chat tools feel faster, clearer, or more capable.
